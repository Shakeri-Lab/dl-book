\section{Vectors, Span, and Basis}

For vectors $v_1,\ldots,v_k$, a linear combination has the form
\[
  a_1v_1+\cdots+a_kv_k.
\]
Their span is the set of all such combinations. A basis is a linearly independent
spanning set. Coordinates describe a vector relative to a declared basis.

\section{Matrices as Maps}

A map $T$ is linear when
\[
  T(\alpha x+\beta y)=\alpha T(x)+\beta T(y).
\]
If $e_1,\ldots,e_n$ is the standard basis, the matrix of $T$ is assembled from the
images of those basis vectors:
\[
  A=\begin{bmatrix}T(e_1)&\cdots&T(e_n)\end{bmatrix},
  \qquad T(x)=Ax.
\]
For example,
\[
\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\-1&3\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}4\\2\end{bmatrix}
=\begin{bmatrix}10\\2\end{bmatrix}.
\]

\section{Composition and Batches}

Applying $A$ and then $B$ gives $B(Ax)=(BA)x$; matrix products generally do not
commute. If examples are stored as rows of $X$, applying the column-vector map
$y=Ax$ to every row gives
\[
  Y=XA^\top.
\]
